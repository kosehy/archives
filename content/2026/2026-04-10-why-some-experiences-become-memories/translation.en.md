# Why do some experiences become memories while others fade away?

Original: https://www.sciencetimes.co.kr/nscvrg/view/menu/251?searchCategory=223&nscvrgSn=261568
Archived: 2026-04-10
Language: English
Type: English translation

At every moment we breathe, we take in tens of thousands of sensory signals. The aroma of coffee in the morning, the outfit of a passerby on the commute, even a line from a news article just read. Yet by the time we go to bed, most of this information has vanished as if by magic, and only a tiny fraction survives in memory. On closer thought, it does not even seem that only especially pleasant information remains.

So by what standard does the brain perform this ruthless act of selection and concentration? According to recent research by neuroscientist Marlene Bartos, the key to this fierce internal editing process lies in the delicate push and pull between two kinds of nerve cells.

## The starring role in recording memory: pyramidal cells

In the hippocampus and cerebral cortex, there are pyramidal cells that actually encode and store information. When outside stimuli arrive, they emit electrical signals and form connection networks with one another. When we say we are studying, building knowledge, or learning a new skill, these pyramidal cells are busy constructing new neural circuits.

But pyramidal cells are, by nature, overenthusiastic. They tend to react to every incoming stimulus. If they tried to record everything without restraint, the brain would quickly fill with useless informational debris. It would be like a textbook in which every line has been highlighted, making the truly important parts impossible to find.

## The cool-headed director filtering the flood: inhibitory neurons

The rescuers here are inhibitory neurons, also called interneurons. They calm the excessive excitement of pyramidal cells and finely regulate the flow of neural signals, acting like supervisors on site. By releasing inhibitory neurotransmitters such as GABA, they suppress meaningless noise from the countless signals emitted by pyramidal cells and allow only important signals to stand out clearly.

According to Bartos’s analysis, remembering something clearly depends not only on pyramidal cells receiving information well, but even more on how effectively inhibitory neurons remove surrounding noise. In that sense, memory is not only the act of recording something, but also the active editing skill of erasing almost everything else.

## The delicate tug-of-war that creates stable memory

The efficiency of learning and the stability of memory depend on how rhythmically these two kinds of cells interact. When pyramidal cells transmit information and inhibitory neurons intervene at the right moment to sharpen the signal’s resolution, that information qualifies to move into long-term memory storage. If this fine coordination fails, the information lingers briefly and then evaporates as short-term memory.

Recent medical-science data suggest that this mechanism is deeply connected not only to learning ability but also to psychiatric conditions such as schizophrenia and anxiety disorders. If inhibitory neurons fail to do their job, uncontrolled noise fills the brain and thinking becomes confused. If they work too strongly, new learning becomes difficult. A healthy brain, then, is one in which these two cell types maintain a precise balance and coolly distinguish what should be recorded from what should be discarded.

## Memory optimization strategies suggested by the data

Taken together, this means that simply stuffing the brain with more information is not the answer. Learning is completed only when the brain is given enough conditions to edit and inhibit information appropriately. This also explains why sufficient rest and sleep are essential for stronger memory. While the brain rests, inhibitory neurons are busy clearing away the day’s informational debris and reinforcing only the important signals.

Next-generation neuroscience is now paying attention to precision medical technologies that could artificially regulate the interaction of these two cell types to treat learning disorders or enhance memory. Understanding this remarkable editing technique of brain cells may become an important milestone in the next leap of human intellectual ability. If there is a precious experience you want to remember today, perhaps it is worth giving your brain a little time to rest so that the inner director can shine the right spotlight on that scene.
