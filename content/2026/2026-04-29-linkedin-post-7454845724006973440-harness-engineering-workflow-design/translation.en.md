Over the past year, while reporting directly to the CEO and receiving feedback, one thing I learned is this: people who work well are not merely people who solve the task immediately in front of them. They are people who can look at work structurally and build systems.

Translated into the AI era, I think it looks like this.

Working well is almost the same as being good at prompting: the ability to create good inputs; the ability to decide what to ask, how to ask it, and how to verify the result within a single turn.

But the kind of person the CEO meant by “someone who can build systems” is not on that layer. It is not someone who writes a good prompt from scratch every time, but someone who creates an environment where good prompts naturally emerge. What information should be placed in memory in advance? What skills should be created? What triggers should run automatically? When a new tool appears, how should the existing environment be rebuilt? It is not just understanding the tool perfectly; it is seeing how the tool redefines one’s work.

In today’s language this is harness engineering. That may not have been the term the CEO intended, but I think it all points to the same idea.

The gap between someone who is good at prompting and someone who can evolve a harness does not widen linearly over time. It compounds. A person who evolves their harness improves their own environment every year, so the same amount of work produces different results.

These days, job descriptions and interview questions often include, “Are you good at using AI? How do you use it?” But I think this question is insufficient. Many people already use AI to some degree.

After several rounds of trial and error, I changed the question to:

- “When was the last time you rebuilt your AI workflow?”
- “What did you throw away then?”
- “Why did you decide that it no longer worked?”

To answer these questions, a person must have objectified their own way of working at least once. Only someone who manages their own workflow can answer them.

People often say, “Korean education is memorization-oriented,” but I do not think that is precise.

What Korean education has evaluated is the ability to execute a fixed procedure quickly within a given problem space. Think of the math section of the CSAT: the ability to apply memorized formulas accurately within a time limit. This is closer to execution than memorization.

That ability itself is not bad. Doctors, lawyers, and accountants all work on top of that ability. Accurate procedural execution can be called the foundation of professional trust.

But if that becomes the entirety of evaluation, something is left empty: the ability to design one’s own learning. Which procedure should be chosen? When a procedure is wrong, should it be rebuilt? How should a new procedure be created? We have never evaluated this.

Some people execute procedures well, and some people know how to create procedures. These days I often think Korean education is optimized to produce the former.
