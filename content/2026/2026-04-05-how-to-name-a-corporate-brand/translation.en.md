# How to Choose a Corporate Brand Name: Trademark Search

## Overview

This post explains how to choose a corporate brand name when starting a corporation in Korea, and how to validate that name through trademark and domain checks.

The author argues that a brand name should not just sound good. It should be legally defensible, available for use in the right category, and ideally matched with a usable domain name.

## What a brand name means

The post defines a brand name as the unified name under which a company presents a representative group of products or services, so that people recognize them as a coherent offering.

The benefit of branding, in this framing, is marketing efficiency: it is often more efficient to promote a unified product or service line under one recognizable identity than to market many offerings separately.

## Recommended process

The article summarizes the process like this:

- come up with at least five candidate brand names aligned with the business purpose
- search the Korean trademark database for duplicate or conflicting marks
- check whether the English-form domain is available and purchase it if possible
- file for trademark registration

## CI and BI distinction

The post also introduces the distinction between:

- CI: corporate identity
- BI: brand identity

Using Samsung as an example, it explains that a company may use one identity at the corporate level and another identity at the product level. Larger companies may split these for strategic, political, or international-market reasons.

## What makes a good brand name

According to the author, a good brand name should:

- be easy for many people to say
- feel familiar
- be memorable
- trigger the intended meaning or image immediately when heard

Examples given as strong names include Baedal Minjok, 11st, and E-Mart.

## Trademark search

The post recommends using KIPRIS (`www.kipris.or.kr`) to check whether someone has already filed or registered a trademark under the same or similar name.

The key point is not just whether the name appears in search results, but whether it overlaps with the same product or service classification relevant to the intended business.

The author notes that trademark registration can take roughly 6 to 12 months after filing.

It also mentions that even if a text mark conflicts, it may sometimes be possible to pursue a logo/image-based filing strategy.

## Domain checks

The post argues that once a brand name is selected, the matching English-form domain should be checked and, if available, reserved in advance.

Suggested checks include:

- `brandname.co.kr`
- `brandname.com`

If the ideal domain is already taken, the article suggests:

- changing the mark
- adding a character before or after the name
- or using variations such as adding `i-` in front

## Compound words and invented words

The author says compound words or newly invented terms have a better chance of being both trademarkable and available as domains.

Simple ordinary words are more likely to have already been claimed by someone else.

## Main takeaway

The central message is that company-name search, trademark search, and domain search should be done together, not separately.

In practice, the founder should prepare multiple name candidates and repeatedly test them across:

- the corporate registry
- the trademark database
- domain registrars

until a name survives all three checks.

## Final advice

The post emphasizes that naming cannot really be outsourced to legal or registration professionals. Attorneys and trademark agents can process filings, but they do not create the founder’s business identity.

That naming step belongs to the business owner and deserves serious thought, because the brand becomes a long-term representation of the business.
