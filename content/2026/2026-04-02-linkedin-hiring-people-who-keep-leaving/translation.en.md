# The people we work so hard to hire keep leaving.

“The people we work so hard to hire keep leaving.”

An HR manager at a startup I had known for some time shared a concern. Even when they persuaded strong candidates to join with great effort, people kept leaving—sometimes in as little as three months, rarely lasting beyond a year. Some did not leave on good terms, which meant negative reviews accumulated on sites like Blind and JobPlanet. Beyond that, the constant cycle of people joining and leaving was shaking the organization and draining the HR manager’s motivation.

“Why are people leaving?”

When I asked, I heard the usual reasons: they were unclear on the company’s direction, the work intensity was high, the future was not visible, and the reality differed from what they had expected.

“How does the CEO see this? Do they feel a real sense of crisis about people leaving after being hired with such difficulty?”

At that, the HR manager’s expression darkened.

“That’s actually why I wanted to speak with you. Strong people keep leaving, but the CEO does not see it as a problem. He says they were simply not the right fit, and that unless you actually work together, interviews alone can’t tell you enough. He just tells me to find another candidate. But applicants contact former employees and ask why they left. It keeps getting harder to bring people of that caliber in.”

After a short pause, the HR manager explained the real reason for asking for a coffee chat.

“If we hired a CHRO, would that person be able to persuade the CEO?”

I laughed and answered: “Of course not.”

—

A high turnover rate does not always mean there is a serious problem. Depending on the company’s stage and direction, the people it needs may change. Both the company and the employee may try to adapt to changing goals, but if they ultimately want different things, it may be better to part ways.

But if that is not the case—if the very people the company wants to keep working with are the ones continuing to leave—and the CEO does not even recognize that as a problem, then no remedy will help.

A CHRO cannot arrive and persuade the CEO. Bringing in a CHRO from outside only has some meaning when the CEO already recognizes the seriousness of the situation, wants to improve it, and simply does not know how. (I say “only some meaning” because people do not change easily.)

Then I turned the question around.

“Why are you still at this company?”

I asked the HR manager directly. If they understood why the people they had worked so hard to hire were leaving, and if they themselves could not understand a CEO who saw no problem in it, why were they still bringing people into that company?

“To be honest, I do understand the CEO. The company is past break-even and he has conviction in the business. The part I do not understand is you. A job change has a major impact on a person’s life. Even if misunderstandings happen and some departures are inevitable, why are you still recruiting people into a company where most leave within a year?”

There was a long silence. I had not asked the question to blame the HR manager, and I worried that I might have hurt them. But I also hoped that someone whose role is to bring people into a company would feel at least that much weight about the responsibility of the work.

An interview, and hiring itself, is not a poker game where each side tries to deceive the other. If you invite someone into a company, you should have enough conviction yourself to do so.
