# Firms Must Stop Buying Software and Start Building Platforms

Source: https://contechroundup.substack.com/p/firms-must-stop-buying-software-and
Saved: 2026-04-19

This Substack essay argues that AI changes the old buy-versus-build calculation for firms, especially in AEC and other expertise-heavy industries. Under traditional SaaS, firms bought software to gain productivity while keeping most of their institutional intelligence inside the firm. Under AI systems, the essay argues, each correction, override, refinement, and workflow interaction can become part of the vendor’s learning loop.

That means, in the author’s framing, firms are no longer just purchasing tools. They may also be contributing their operational intelligence to systems that can later generalize that expertise across the wider market.

## Core thesis

The central claim is that firms should stop treating vertical AI adoption as a straightforward software procurement decision and instead think in terms of what must be rented versus what must be owned. The author argues that the real strategic question is where institutional knowledge, domain judgment, and decision logic should live.

In this model, horizontal AI infrastructure such as foundation models, OCR, embeddings, orchestration frameworks, and observability platforms can be bought because they are commoditizing rapidly. The vertical layer, however, should be built or at least tightly controlled internally, because it is where a firm’s proprietary expertise is encoded.

## Why the old buy model is said to be breaking

The piece says the old model worked when software was mostly passive. Firms used the tool, captured efficiency gains, and vendors improved products based on roadmap priorities rather than deep operational learning from every user action.

AI changes that. In the essay’s argument, when specialists correct AI outputs, refine summaries, or intervene in domain workflows, they are not only using software but also training it. This creates a bidirectional value exchange in which the vendor can accumulate expertise from many customers and embed what was once a firm-specific advantage into a shared system.

The author treats this as a structural strategic risk, especially in sectors where expertise, liability, and tacit operational judgment matter deeply.

## Main risks highlighted

The essay emphasizes several risks from relying too heavily on external vertical AI systems:

- institutional knowledge migrates into external agentic workflows
- firms lose visibility into how decisions are produced
- accountability remains with licensed professionals even when the tool is opaque
- vendors may accumulate and standardize competitive expertise across the market
- siloed vertical tools create fragmented accountability and weak auditability

A key theme is that firms can end up retaining legal and professional responsibility while operating below systems they do not fully understand or control.

## Buy the horizontal, build the vertical

The article condenses its recommendation into a simple strategic split:

- **Buy the Horizontal**: foundation models, OCR, embeddings, orchestration, observability, and similar general-purpose infrastructure
- **Build the Vertical**: workflows, internal logic, standards, risk tolerances, and decision systems tuned to the firm’s actual operations

The author stresses that this internal platform is not imagined as starting from a blank-sheet software project. Instead, it begins in close consulting-style engagement with real client or project problems, and only later gets crystallized into repeatable systems.

## What firms should own

The essay identifies four layers firms should build around and own:

### 1. Proprietary Data (Memory)

Project history, productivity records, installation sequences, supplier performance, schedule logic changes, and other operational memory.

### 2. Operational Workflows (Execution)

Estimating methods, procurement playbooks, change management, risk escalation, RFI triage, and other delivery processes that often live as tacit knowledge inside experienced teams.

### 3. Integration Architecture (Coordination)

The connective layer across drawings, specifications, schedules, cost codes, identifiers, metadata standards, permissions, versioning, and audit trails.

### 4. Decision Logic (Judgment)

Risk scoring, non-compliance detection, forecasting rules, constructibility checks, exception thresholds, and similar rules that convert information into action.

The essay argues these layers compound on each other. Better data sharpens decision logic, better logic improves workflows, and better workflows generate richer data. But that feedback loop only compounds for the firm if those layers are collocated inside infrastructure the firm governs.

## Broader strategic framing

The piece uses analogies such as Kodak versus Fujifilm to argue that buying vertical AI tools may repeat an old mistake: treating a systemic technological shift as just another product purchase. By contrast, building a platform for creating, evaluating, and replacing AI components over time is presented as the more adaptive path.

It also claims that AI enables “coordination without consensus,” meaning firms may not need everyone on the same standardized tool stack if AI can interpret heterogeneous inputs and synthesize operational understanding across them.

## Technology leadership implications

A final major claim is that the CIO or technology leader’s role shifts from managing software licenses to governing agentic workflows on top of a proprietary platform. In that model, the unit of management is no longer the application, but the workflow and the system of intelligence around it.

The essay suggests a capability divide is emerging between firms that build and customize AI systems around their own workflows and firms that merely consume vendor-provided interfaces.
