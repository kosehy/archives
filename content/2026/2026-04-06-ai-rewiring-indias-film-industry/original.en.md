## Core framing

The article argues that India’s film industry is reorganizing itself around AI as a response to tightening economics, changing audience behavior, and the search for scalable production efficiency.

## Industry pressure as the trigger

India is the world’s most prolific film producer, but the economics of the business have become less stable. Audience behavior has shifted with the rise of streaming, cinema attendance has fallen from pre-pandemic levels, and revenue has become increasingly dependent on a smaller number of hits and higher ticket prices.

In that environment, studios are adopting AI not as a novelty but as a practical operating tool to reduce cost, compress timelines, and create new revenue opportunities.

## Where AI is being used

The article describes multiple forms of AI deployment in India’s film industry:

- fully AI-generated or AI-heavy productions, especially in mythology and fantasy genres
- AI-assisted multilingual dubbing to reach fragmented language markets
- AI re-edits and rewritten endings for older titles to create new commercial releases
- broader experimentation inside studio pipelines as major production houses build internal AI capability

One studio executive says AI can reduce production costs to one-fifth and production time to one-quarter of traditional filmmaking levels in some genres.

## Why India looks different from Hollywood

The report contrasts India with Hollywood.

In Hollywood, AI use is constrained by union agreements, consent rules, and broader concerns over labor displacement. In India, studios appear more willing to move aggressively, both because of fewer institutional constraints and because the economics of the local industry create stronger incentives to experiment.

This difference may allow India to become a major center of AI-enabled filmmaking if execution quality improves.

## Example: rewriting older films

One example in the article is the AI-modified re-release of the 2013 film _Raanjhanaa_, whose tragic ending was replaced with a happier one.

The move triggered criticism from the lead actor, who said the remake stripped the film of its soul and set a troubling precedent for art and artists. Even so, the re-release still attracted audience demand, and the studio said it is reviewing its large back catalog for more AI-assisted adaptation opportunities.

This captures the economic tension at the center of the article: commercial viability and cultural legitimacy may diverge.

## Example: AI mythology and episodic content

The article also highlights large-scale AI work in mythology-based content, a commercially important category in India.

Studios are planning multiple AI-generated titles around deities and epic material, and JioStar has already aired an AI-generated adaptation of _Mahabharat_. The series appears to have reached meaningful view counts, but audience reception has been rough, with criticism around lip sync, styling, and authenticity.

This suggests that scale and cost efficiency are already possible, even if artistic acceptance remains unresolved.

## Dubbing as a more viable near-term use case

Compared with full AI-generated storytelling, the article presents AI dubbing as a more immediately practical application.

India’s linguistic fragmentation makes dubbing central to national film reach, and AI tools are beginning to improve lip sync and naturalness across languages. This is a narrower but potentially more commercially sustainable use of AI because it solves a concrete distribution problem.

## Big tech’s role

The article notes that global technology companies such as Google and Microsoft are already partnering with Indian filmmakers and studios.

That matters because it suggests AI adoption in film is not just a local studio experiment. It is becoming part of a broader industry stack that links studios, tooling vendors, cloud infrastructure, and generative media systems.

## Final takeaway

The article’s main argument is that India’s film industry is not merely testing AI on the edges. It is beginning to reorganize production, distribution, and library monetization around it.

The opportunity is clear: lower costs, faster output, broader linguistic reach, and new life for old catalogs.

The unresolved question is whether audiences will accept AI-mediated cinema as economically useful, artistically legitimate, or both.
