## Secondary preservation summary of the linked article

According to the accessible GeekNews preservation text, fatherhood is associated not only with behavioral change after childbirth but also with hormonal shifts and neurological adaptation, and these changes appear stronger when fathers are more actively involved in caregiving.

The preserved summary highlights repeated findings that fathers often show lower testosterone than non-fathers, and that lower testosterone during a partner’s pregnancy is associated with greater caregiving involvement after birth. It also describes observed changes in oxytocin, prolactin, and vasopressin, especially when fathers have more contact, play, and early bonding with the child.

The preserved source also reports research comparing men before and after the birth of a first child, suggesting neurological adaptation to new caregiving tasks and experiences. It notes larger shifts in men who reported stronger prenatal bonding or planned longer parental leave.

A final major theme is policy relevance. The preserved material argues that early paternal involvement and family policy support matter, and cites studies linking active father participation to maternal mental health and child cardiovascular health.

## Main points recoverable from the accessible preservation text

### Hormonal changes and caregiving readiness

- Across multiple mammals, testosterone, vasopressin, and prolactin shifts are associated with active caregiving.
- Human studies also found fathers tending to have lower testosterone than childless men.
- A Cebu City longitudinal study found that men who became fathers showed significantly lower testosterone than non-fathers, with larger declines among those more engaged in infant care.
- Lower testosterone during a partner’s pregnancy was linked in other studies to greater post-birth involvement, commitment, and responsiveness.

### Changes beginning before birth

- One research group expected the shift to begin after post-birth interaction, but reported measurable hormonal changes already around month four of pregnancy among expectant fathers.
- Lower testosterone and vasopressin during pregnancy were associated with greater later involvement with mother and baby.
- The mechanism remains unclear, with possible explanations including pheromonal cues from the pregnant partner or psychological transition triggered by anticipation of fatherhood.

### Oxytocin, prolactin, and vasopressin

- Oxytocin increases were observed in fathers as well, especially with more time, touch, and play with the child.
- Nasal oxytocin experiments showed altered interaction patterns during father-infant interaction.
- A self-reinforcing loop is suggested: more interaction may raise oxytocin, which in turn may encourage more interaction.
- Prolactin and vasopressin are also discussed as hormones associated with paternal caregiving patterns.

### Brain changes and paternal transition

- Brain-scan research around first-time fatherhood suggests neurological adaptation to caregiving-related novelty and demands.
- Some researchers compare the transition to a developmental window somewhat analogous to adolescence: a period when the brain adapts to new stimuli, tasks, and roles.
- Greater prenatal attachment and longer parental leave plans were associated with larger observed shifts.
- The overall pattern is described as “use it or lose it,” with more active paternal involvement linked to stronger change.

### Family policy and caregiving capacity

- The preserved text emphasizes that human paternal care capacity may be more plastic than older stereotypes assumed.
- Research comparing heterosexual couples and gay male primary caregivers is described as evidence that caregiving role can reorganize paternal brain circuitry.
- The discussion concludes that paternal biology should be taken more seriously in family policy, including parental leave and early participation structures.
