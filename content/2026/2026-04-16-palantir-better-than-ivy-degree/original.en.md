# Palantir CEO says working at his $316B software company is better than a degree from Harvard or Yale

Source: https://fortune.com/article/billionaire-palantir-ceo-alex-karp-working-at-his-tech-firm-better-than-harvard-yale-princeton-degree-does-not-matter-gen-z/
Saved: 2026-04-16

With Gen Z facing an uphill battle in today’s job market, and many facing heavy student loan debt, a growing number of young people have concluded that pursuing a degree may have been a poor investment. Some business leaders are increasingly agreeing.

Great Place to Work CEO Michael Bush previously told Fortune that top employers are no longer really talking about degrees and are instead talking about skills.

Palantir CEO Alex Karp is among the executives publicly questioning the value of traditional schooling.

“If you did not go to school, or you went to a school that’s not that great, or you went to Harvard or Princeton or Yale, once you come to Palantir, you’re a Palantirian, no one cares about the other stuff,” Karp said during an August 2025 earnings call.

Karp added that his company is building a new credential separate from class or background and said that working at Palantir is the best credential in tech.

## Palantir’s growth story and internal ideology

The article says Palantir is pulling in revenue near or above $1 billion each quarter and that its stock price rose more than 100% in 2025. Its market cap is described as more than $316 billion at the time of publication, though below a prior peak above $475 billion.

According to Karp, Palantir’s rise is not due to luxury headquarters or Ivy League recruiting, but to attracting people who are motivated by consequential work rather than elite educational prestige.

Palantir CTO Shyam Sankar echoed this framing, saying the company attracts and retains people who want to “bend the arc of history” by working on problems that drive outcomes.

The article connects this posture to Palantir’s broader skepticism toward existing education and talent-development systems. It notes that Karp, Peter Thiel, and Joe Lonsdale have backed the University of Austin, described as a newer school built around free speech and an explicitly anti-woke ethos.

## Hiring younger talent while criticizing higher education

The article notes that Palantir continues to hire across product development, government roles, internships, and new graduate pipelines.

It also highlights Palantir’s Meritocracy Fellowship, a four-month paid internship for high school graduates reconsidering higher education. Admission is described as being based on merit and academic excellence, though applicants are still expected to present extremely high standardized test scores, such as at least 1460 on the SAT or 33 on the ACT.

The internship is framed by the company as a response to perceived shortcomings in university admissions. The Fortune article quotes Palantir’s posting as saying that opaque admissions standards have displaced meritocracy and excellence, and that campuses have become breeding grounds for extremism and chaos.

The article also cites Karp telling CNBC that everything many people learned in school and college about how the world works is intellectually incorrect.

The internship’s message is summarized by the slogan: “Skip the debt. Skip the indoctrination. Get the Palantir Degree.”

## Contradiction: hiring youth while pursuing AI efficiency

The article ends by highlighting a tension. The same young talent Palantir wants to attract may be helping to build systems that reduce the need for workers.

Karp reportedly said he hopes to reduce the workforce by 500 employees while continuing to grow revenue. He described the company’s direction as a highly efficient revolution, with the goal of achieving much higher revenue with a smaller headcount.
