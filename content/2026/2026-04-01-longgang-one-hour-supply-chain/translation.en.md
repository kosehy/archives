# Longgang’s “One-Hour Supply Chain”

Saved: 2026-04-01
Translation note: Smoothed into natural English for readability while preserving the structure and argument of the article.

Longgang, in Guangdong Province, is an administrative district of Shenzhen. Officially called Longgang District, it is a relatively small district by Chinese standards, covering just 388 square kilometers and located about 30 to 40 minutes northeast of central Shenzhen.

The more closely one examines Longgang, the harder it is to underestimate it. Longgang represents Chinese manufacturing at the district level. It is effectively a flagship manufacturing zone. In 2024 it ranked first in China’s “Top 100 Manufacturing Districts,” selected by the China Academy of Information and Communications Technology, and it has held that position continuously since 2017.

Its corporate base explains much of that strength. Huawei, one of China’s leading ICT firms, is based there, as is BYD, a central force in the electric-vehicle boom. The district alone hosts 48 listed companies, many in semiconductors, e-commerce, biotech, and AI.

These companies have built what is called a “one-hour supply chain,” meaning that all necessary parts can be sourced within one hour. A phone call is enough for components to arrive quickly. That is why observers say anyone who wants to understand China’s supply chain should look at Longgang.

Huawei played a major role in creating this system. After settling there in the 1990s, it built a network of about 2,000 related suppliers within a 30-kilometer radius. Roughly 80% of the parts needed for smartphones and telecom equipment are sourced from Longgang and the broader Shenzhen area. Circuit boards come from Shennan Circuit, LED displays from Absen, and smart displays from Konka subsidiaries and related firms—all based in Longgang. The result is something close to a fully formed smart cluster.

According to Feng Tiejun, director of Longgang District’s Enterprise Service Center, Huawei’s local procurement in Longgang amounts to between 100 and 150 billion yuan, generating an estimated more than 100,000 jobs. The cluster therefore strengthens both firms and the local economy.

BYD grew on top of this cluster but took a different path from Huawei. While Huawei relied heavily on surrounding suppliers, BYD concentrated on vertical integration, directly developing and producing key components such as batteries.

That strategy paid off. Under similar production conditions, BYD’s cost base is estimated to be 15% to 20% lower than competitors’ and its completion speed about three times faster. If other companies need five days to procure components externally, BYD can place an order in the afternoon and put it into production the next day. This sharply reduces inventory and logistics costs. BYD’s vertical-integration ratio is estimated at 70% to 80%, compared with Tesla’s 60% to 70% and Hyundai’s 40% to 50%.

At the same time, BYD’s strategy was only possible because Longgang already offered such a dense supply-chain base. Shenzhen as a whole has more than 150 industrial supply chains, ranging from shoes to cars to advanced flying vehicles. A car requires around 20,000 parts, and BYD can source about 90% of those parts from Longgang and nearby areas.

## Industrial strategy in Longgang and Shenzhen

Longgang follows an “11+2” strategy, under which the district government strategically fosters 11 emerging industries such as communications, semiconductors, and displays, as well as two future industries: the low-altitude economy and AI. Firms in these fields receive various benefits, and the cluster continues to deepen through that process.

Seen from the broader Shenzhen level, the cluster becomes even larger. Besides Longgang, Shenzhen includes multiple other districts with their own concentrations of industry: Nanshan with Tencent and DJI, Longhua with Foxconn and Meituan facilities, Futian as a financial hub, Bao’an with broad manufacturing, and Guangming with national-level research centers. Shoes, TVs, smartphones, and electric vehicles are all produced within Shenzhen.

If Longgang has “11+2,” Shenzhen has “20+8,” referring to 20 emerging industries and 8 future industries designated for concentrated support. In this sense, Shenzhen is evolving from “China’s Silicon Valley” into “Asia’s Silicon Valley.”

Still, Shenzhen does not dominate every dimension. It is often judged to lag Beijing in R&D capacity. UBTech, a globally known robotics company headquartered in Shenzhen, keeps its humanoid robotics headquarters in Beijing because of Beijing’s stronger AI, autonomous-driving, university, and research ecosystem centered around Zhongguancun.

Similarly, China’s semiconductor ecosystem is forming around Shanghai and the Yangtze River Delta. Shanghai hosts major firms such as SMIC and the equipment company SMEE, while nearby Hefei and Nanjing host memory and advanced packaging firms. The goal is to create an end-to-end semiconductor supply chain spanning design, manufacturing, and post-processing.

Taken together, China is building specialized industrial clusters across different cities, each aligned with local strengths. Government designs the cluster structure, and firms respond within that framework.

## China’s “chain leader system”

A distinctive institutional mechanism behind this is the “chain leader system” (链长制).

In Chinese, supply chain is “gongyinglian,” where “lian” means chain. A “chain leader” therefore functions like a chief chain officer responsible for managing and strengthening a regional supply chain. This system is designed to align government and business around supply-chain construction.

Three actors are central to the model. First is the chain leader, usually a senior government official such as a provincial governor, mayor, or district head, responsible for policy design. Second is the “chain owner,” the core enterprise in the supply chain, which identifies concrete market needs. Third is the “chain office,” the implementation body inside government that connects policy and enterprise demand.

Together, these actors build, fill gaps in, extend, and strengthen supply chains. Hangzhou’s semiconductor ecosystem is cited as one example, where city leadership, development zones, and firms such as SMIC work together to create a full chain from raw materials to design, fabrication, and packaging.

Guangdong is described as one of the places where this system works best. Local officials say that the provincial governor even acts personally as a “general chain leader.” According to Longgang officials, this kind of top-level provincial support was crucial in enabling the district’s one-hour supply chain.

China is now making a full-scale push for supply-chain self-reliance in high-tech sectors. The U.S.-China technological rivalry is accelerating this effort. As central government, local government, firms, universities, and research institutes all participate, supply chains are becoming denser and stronger, and industrial clusters are spreading across major cities. Longgang’s one-hour supply chain is portrayed as one of the clearest starting points of this broader movement.
