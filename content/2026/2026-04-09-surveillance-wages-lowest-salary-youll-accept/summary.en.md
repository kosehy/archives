# Summary — Employers are using your personal data to figure out the lowest salary you'll accept

## Core idea

This article argues that data-driven pricing logic is spreading from consumer markets into labor markets. Employers and labor-tech vendors are increasingly able to use personal data and behavioral signals to estimate the lowest wage a worker is likely to accept.

## Key points

- The article describes this trend as "surveillance wages," an extension of surveillance pricing.
- Experts say some systems may use financial-vulnerability signals, behavioral history, location, or public social data to estimate a worker's minimum acceptable pay.
- The practice may affect hiring, wage increases, bonuses, and platform-based shift pricing.
- Gig healthcare staffing and rideshare platforms are presented as especially visible examples of algorithmically individualized pay.
- Critics argue this can create discrimination, opacity, and long-term wage compression for vulnerable workers.
- Some lawmakers are beginning to respond, including a Colorado bill aimed at banning the use of intimate personal data in algorithmic wage-setting.

## One-line takeaway

The article warns that employers may increasingly use surveillance-style data practices not only to charge consumers more, but also to pay workers less.
