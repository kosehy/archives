## One-paragraph summary
This article traces the “grayening” of modern material culture, arguing that the spread of gray, beige, black, and white across houses, cars, devices, and interiors is not just a taste shift but the combined result of industrial standardization, modernist design ideology, resale economics, and aspirational minimalism. What looks like neutral aesthetics is really a long-running cultural and commercial convergence toward low-risk color choices. The piece ends by suggesting that this cycle may be peaking, with warmer and bolder colors beginning to re-emerge.

## Key points
- Neutral colors became dominant partly because they are easier to standardize, sell globally, and resell later.
- Modernist thinkers and designers gave desaturated palettes philosophical prestige by framing ornament and color as unserious or primitive.
- The auto industry and housing market reinforced grayscale through production efficiency and resale logic.
- There are signs that the trend may be reversing as richer colors return in design and automotive palettes.

## Memorable takeaway
Gray won because it became the safest common language of industrial culture, not because people suddenly stopped liking color.
