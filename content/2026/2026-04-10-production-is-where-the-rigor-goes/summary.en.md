## One-paragraph summary

Charity Majors argues that current discussions about where engineering rigor moves in the AI era still underweight the one place closest to reality: production. Her point is that observability should not be treated as a bug-finding tool or incident-only concern, but as the core feedback mechanism that connects developer intent, system behavior, and user outcomes. As AI accelerates the rate of change, pre-production checks alone stop being enough, and teams need richer telemetry, tighter production feedback loops, and validation in the live system itself.

## Key points

- The article critiques AI-era rigor discussions for emphasizing specs, tests, and constraints while barely naming production as the central site of truth.
- Observability is framed as understanding, not just monitoring, and production telemetry is presented as the reality check that survived contact with users.
- AI raises the speed of code change so dramatically that informal human intuition and duct-tape processes no longer scale.
- The author argues that meaningful rigor now requires production instrumentation, fast feedback loops, and outcome-based validation rather than relying only on pre-release testing.

## Memorable takeaway

Production is not where rigor ends, it is where rigor has to live.
