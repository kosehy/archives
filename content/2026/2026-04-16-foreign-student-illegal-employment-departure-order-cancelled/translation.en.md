## Subtitle

At the boundary between a single violation and immigration status

A case involved an international student who was caught working as a day laborer at a construction site for just one day. The person explained it as a matter of simple livelihood, but legally it clearly counted as an activity outside the permitted scope of status, in other words, illegal employment. Immigration authorities immediately imposed cancellation of status and a departure order.

The first point to examine in this case is that illegal employment is not merely an administrative violation. It can affect a person’s entire future immigration status. This is especially important for someone considering a change to a D-10 job-seeking visa or an E-7 professional work visa, because a prior violation record becomes a major factor in the review.

Under the relevant standards, visa issuance or status change may be restricted if there has been a criminal penalty or administrative fine within a certain period counted from the application date. For example, a fine of at least three million won within the past three years, or an administrative penalty above a certain level, may itself restrict immigration permission. Illegal employment is itself subject to an administrative penalty, and the amount and duration can weigh negatively in later visa screening.

In practice, more than the mere fact of detection, four factors are considered together:

1. duration of the violation
2. amount of the penalty
3. whether it was repeated
4. degree of intent

In this case, the violation lasted only one day, and the penalty was relatively low. There was no prior record of legal violations, and it was confirmed that the person immediately admitted the violation and completed payment after being caught.

The main issue then became whether the immigration authority’s disposition was actually appropriate.

A departure order is a serious measure because it forcibly ends a foreign national’s stay, so it is not automatically justified simply because a violation occurred.

The court reviewed the case under the principle of proportionality.

In other words, it examined whether the disposition was appropriate by comparing the public interest with the personal disadvantage that the individual would suffer.

Specifically, the court placed weight on the following factors:

First, the violation lasted only one day.

Second, the person immediately admitted the wrongdoing and accepted the sanction.

Third, the person had substantial ties to Korean society, including continuation of study, completion of social integration programs, and formation of family relationships.

Considering these circumstances together, the court held that the disadvantage to the individual was excessively large compared with the public interest that could be achieved by the departure order. As a result, the disposition was cancelled as an abuse or deviation of administrative discretion.

Still, it would be risky to generalize from this case.

As a practical rule, when there is a record of illegal employment, changing to a D-10 or E-7 visa is ordinarily heavily restricted. In particular, if the penalty exceeds two million won, if the violation period is long, or if the conduct was repeated, a change of status becomes practically very difficult.

In addition, recent practice includes cases in which illegal employment is discovered afterward through income certificates or tax records, so even if a person has not yet been caught, there is no safe basis for complacency.

Ultimately, this case shows two things at the same time.

First, illegal employment is a serious violation that can critically affect immigration status. Second, in exceptional cases, the severity of the violation and the person’s circumstances can be considered together so that the disposition may be adjusted.

But the latter remains a limited exception.

In most cases, even a single violation changes the entire future immigration plan.

For that reason, any foreign national considering a change in immigration status needs to think very carefully about how a short-term choice may affect long-term immigration stability.
