## Woongjae Lee’s Post

A dumpling shop in China has published an agent skill.

It is Jinguyuan, a 20-year-old dumpling restaurant in front of Beijing University of Posts and Telecommunications. Once they attached a single AI Skill plugin, people could simply say to their AI assistant, “What time are they open until?”, “Do they have seats?”, or “Order dumplings for delivery.” The AI could handle location lookup, checking business hours, wait numbers, delivery orders, and even instructions on how to wrap dumplings.

China is pushing this at the national level. There are already hundreds of millions of people in China using AI assistants. ByteDance’s Doubao has already exceeded 300 million users, and Tencent Yuanbao has passed 100 million. These people are talking to AI every day.

Shenzhen has even introduced subsidies for AI agent businesses, and shopping malls now advertise services like “We install OpenClaw for you, it only takes 30 minutes.” As a result, OpenClaw reportedly reached 20 million users in a single month earlier this year. As ordinary people begin using AI agents in daily life, even neighborhood dumpling shops are starting to build interfaces for AI.

Our own company is now working with Claude Code across the whole organization, and we are also working with customers who use Claude Code. Because of that, buttons are starting to disappear from our product. People issue upload and download commands to the product through Claude Code, and the product mainly just confirms them. As more work gets done through agents, the shape of the interface itself is starting to change.

Right now, humans are the customers, so products still take a human-oriented form. But once AI starts to become the customer, the form of the product will have to change as well. What the dumpling shop in China shows is not just technology adoption. It may be a signal that the underlying assumption about who products exist for is changing.
