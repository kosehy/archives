# China emphasizes a “token economy”... “Convert abundant electricity into tokens and export them to the world”

Original: https://www.aitimes.com/news/articleView.html?idxno=208626
Saved: 2026-04-01
Translation note: Smoothed into natural English for readability while preserving the article’s claims and structure.

The Chinese government has identified “tokens” as both a new growth indicator and a new export category. The idea is to reorganize China’s digital economy through the explosive growth of AI.

On the 24th, China’s National Data Administration said daily token consumption had exceeded 140 trillion as of March. That is more than 1,000 times higher than the 100 billion recorded in early 2024, and more than 40% higher than the 100 trillion reported at the end of last year.

Liu Liehong, head of the National Data Administration, said the surge shows that China’s AI industry is expanding rapidly and evolving from basic chat functions toward more sophisticated systems capable of decision-making and task execution.

This rapid growth is attributed in part to the recent “OpenClaw” wave spreading across China and to the rise of vibe coding driven by improvements in open-source models.

Major Chinese AI companies have recently rolled out tools that allow users to use OpenClaw on their own platforms. The goal is to increase the utilization of their in-house models. Agents such as OpenClaw also generate far more tokens than conventional short-answer AI chatbots.

China’s leading AI companies are also releasing major models that are much cheaper than American models while approaching them in capability. As a result, on OpenRouter’s weekly AI token usage rankings last month, Chinese models including MiniMax, DeepSeek, and Moonshot AI ranked near the top, with MiniMax taking first place. It was the first time Chinese models had overtaken U.S. models on that platform.

Coding usage is especially token-intensive compared with chatbot use. While a chatbot may need roughly 30,000 tokens to summarize Shakespeare’s Hamlet, an AI agent may require up to 20 million tokens even for a simple coding task.

The Chinese government’s announcement reflects this broader environment. Liu said the government will push policies to build a unified national system for data property-right registration, create an integrated national data market, and promote data infrastructure development. He emphasized that the increasingly discussed idea of “token exports” is a visible indicator of these changes.

At the core of this trend is a new economic logic. China aims to build computing capacity using its vast electricity production and then sell low-cost AI tokens to the world. Electricity exported from China currently trades at about 0.5 yuan ($0.07) per kilowatt-hour, but one analysis suggests that converting that energy into AI tokens could raise its value by as much as 22 times.

The Chinese government has even proposed giving tokens an official name, “Ciyuan,” in an attempt to brand them as a new economic unit.

Wang Peng, a researcher at the Beijing Academy of Social Sciences, said this approach could lower barriers to entry for users around the world. He argued that competitively priced tokens would allow small businesses, independent developers, and users in emerging markets to access AI tools that had previously been difficult to reach.

Western China in particular offers abundant low-cost renewable energy. China has also built a vertically integrated supply chain spanning ultra-high-voltage transmission, liquid-cooled data centers, and server assembly, which the article says creates a combination that competitors will find difficult to match.

Shi Weixia, a senior engineer at the China Academy of Information and Communications Technology, said China’s token cost advantage over overseas rivals is not simply about electricity prices, but the combined result of energy-cost advantages, stronger AI capabilities, and supply-chain control.
