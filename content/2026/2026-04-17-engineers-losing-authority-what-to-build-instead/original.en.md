# Engineers Losing Authority? Here's What To Build Instead

Source: https://thrivinginengineering.substack.com/p/engineers-losing-authority-heres-what-to-build-instead
Saved: 2026-04-17

This piece presents a curated engineering leadership digest framed around a subtle shift in engineering teams. Its central claim is that authority on engineering teams is not disappearing, but changing form, especially as AI reduces reliance on senior engineers for troubleshooting and traditional displays of technical expertise.

The digest argues that this shift should be treated less as a threat and more as an opportunity to lead differently. The proposed response is for senior engineers and engineering leaders to build authority on foundations AI cannot easily replicate: anticipating future challenges, understanding organizational context, and building cross-team consensus.

## Main thread: rebuilding senior engineering authority

The article highlights Stephane Moreau’s argument that AI has reduced dependence on senior engineers for troubleshooting and decision-making, revealing how fragile authority can be when it depends mostly on technical problem-solving. The recommended response is not defensive resistance, but a redirection toward higher-order influence skills.

The digest frames this as an important leadership issue because engineering managers need to help teams move away from valuing firefighting alone and toward valuing prevention, judgment, and influence without formal authority.

## Leadership path choices

The article also summarizes Ryan Murphy’s discussion of the difference between becoming a Tech Lead and becoming an Engineering Manager. It emphasizes that these are distinct career paths rather than a simple hierarchy, and that both require a shift away from direct coding toward forms of influence, mentorship, and team stewardship.

## Broader industry discussions included in the digest

The digest then moves through a series of linked discussions and interviews:

- Martin Fowler and Kent Beck, in conversation with Gergely Orosz, on software engineering evolution, agile, AI, and the need for curiosity over certainty.
- Jamin Ball on the value of AI wrappers or harnesses, arguing that orchestration layers around models may matter as much as the models themselves.
- Erik Torenberg, Steven Sinofsky, Martin Casado, and Aaron Levie on the AI adoption gap in enterprises, legacy software constraints, agent-driven software design, and changing business models.
- Etienne de Bruin on AI-accelerated entropy in codebases and the need for CTOs to become champions of order rather than just acceleration.
- John Cutler on the “Demand Funnel” and the importance of shaping incoming work instead of merely reacting to it.
- Parth Oza on leading large, high-pressure engineering organizations without burning teams out.
- A story about GitLab co-founder Sid Sijbrandij and the use of AI in personalized cancer treatment research.
- Andy Budd on designer archetypes, urging more empathy and better organizational support rather than dismissive categorization.

## Themes running across the digest

Although the digest covers many different pieces, several common themes stand out:

- engineering authority is shifting from technical heroics toward judgment and coordination
- AI changes the value of different roles, but does not eliminate the need for leadership
- the orchestration around AI systems may be more strategically important than model choice alone
- engineering leaders increasingly need to manage entropy, demand, collaboration, and organizational context
- high performance now depends as much on systems thinking and alignment as on raw technical output
