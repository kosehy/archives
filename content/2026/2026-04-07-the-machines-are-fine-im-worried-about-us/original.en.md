# The machines are fine. I'm worried about us.

Source: https://ergosphere.blog/posts/the-machines-are-fine/
Archived: 2026-04-07
Language: English
Type: Reflective essay / AI in science / academic training

## Core argument

This essay argues that the deepest risk of AI agents in academic research is not that machines will become too capable, but that humans may stop developing the understanding that scientific training is supposed to produce.

Its central fear is not automation itself, but cognitive outsourcing during the formative years of becoming an independent researcher.

## Alice and Bob

The essay builds its argument through a contrast between two hypothetical PhD students in astrophysics.

Both students complete similarly scoped projects.
Both publish solid papers.
From the outside, their year looks identical.

But one of them, Bob, secretly relied on an AI agent for:
- summarizing papers
- explaining new methods
- debugging code
- writing the final paper

The other, Alice, learned the hard way through confusion, repetition, and failure.

The institution sees equivalent output.
The essay argues that the actual outcome is profoundly different.

Alice ends the year with durable internal capability:
- she can read new papers with effort and follow them
- she can write methods from scratch
- she can detect when a result looks wrong
- she has built understanding inside herself

Bob, by contrast, may have shipped the same product without developing the same underlying competence.

That distinction is the heart of the essay: identical research outputs do not imply identical scientific formation.

## Science as training, not only production

The essay leans on David Hogg’s argument that in a field like astrophysics, people are the ends, not the means.

The point of many graduate projects is not merely to obtain the result. It is to produce a scientist through the process of doing difficult work.

Because astrophysics has little direct practical output compared with fields like medicine, the essay argues that the training process is the thing that matters most. If AI agents replace that process, then the only irreplaceable part of the enterprise is what gets removed.

## The Schwartz example

The essay discusses Matthew Schwartz’s experiment supervising Claude through a real theoretical physics calculation.

The article’s interpretation is subtle:
- the experiment shows AI can help produce publishable work
- but it also shows that expert supervision is what actually makes the result scientific

Schwartz succeeds not because the AI independently knows the physics, but because Schwartz knows what to check, what looks suspicious, and what kinds of errors matter.

So the essay argues that stronger models do not eliminate the need for human expertise. They may instead make it harder to notice where real understanding is still required.

## The real danger: slow erosion of understanding

The author rejects both simplistic poles in the debate:
- full embrace, where humans mainly curate machine output
- full prohibition, where AI use is treated as if it can simply be banned away

Instead, the essay claims the real danger is quieter.

It is the gradual normalization of producing acceptable-looking work without understanding what one is doing.

That means a generation of researchers who can:
- generate results
- pass peer review
- use tools effectively

but cannot:
- derive the method from first principles
- explain why the method works
- detect deep errors independently
- supervise others with real confidence

## Friction as curriculum

One of the essay’s strongest ideas is that what people call “grunt work” is often actually the training process itself.

The boring parts are not separable from the important parts in advance.

Debugging, failed derivations, confusion, re-reading, and incorrect attempts are not incidental overhead. They are how tacit understanding gets built.

The essay insists that:
- failures are the curriculum
- error messages are the syllabus
- time spent confused is often what builds the infrastructure of thought needed later for original work

In this view, removing friction too early does not just make work faster. It removes the developmental process that gives future expertise its depth.

## Tool use vs. cognitive outsourcing

The essay does not argue against AI use in general.

It explicitly allows for tool-like use cases:
- syntax help
- phrasing assistance
- formatting help
- lightweight lookup

The critical line is between using a machine to accelerate execution after thinking has already occurred, versus using the machine to bypass the thinking itself.

That line is described as the most important distinction in the entire debate.

## Incentives and rational behavior

The essay is careful not to portray students like Bob as irrational.

From within academia’s incentives, outsourcing thinking may be perfectly rational:
- publication volume matters
- hiring is brutally competitive
- short-term output is rewarded

The problem is that the same system that rewards early publication eventually demands exactly the kind of deep judgment that cannot be skipped during training.

So the essay argues that the danger is not stupidity, but a rational response to a broken incentive structure.

## Final takeaway

The essay’s conclusion is that the machines are not the main problem.

The main problem is the possibility that humans will adapt to these systems by quietly giving up the slow, difficult, and formative work through which understanding is built.

The result would be a scientific culture with preserved outputs but degraded minds.

## Notes on preservation

- The article is essayistic, not neutral. It is grounded in academic training concerns more than a general-purpose AI policy framework.
- The Alice/Bob story is the essay’s key explanatory device and should be read as the central lens for interpreting the whole piece.
